Arriving in Hong Kong, the first thing that hits you is the movement. Trains, trams, ferries, escalators carved into hillsides — everything in motion, everything on time. Chaotic in scale, impossibly precise in execution.

## The markets

Night markets running under strings of red and white light. Vendors waiting, calling out, never pushy — just ready. Fruit stacked in pyramids, fish on ice, clothes hanging in rows three deep. The kind of place where you point and nod and something appears.

## Tai O

A ferry, then a bus, and suddenly the city is gone. Tai O sits on stilts over the water on the far side of Lantau — a fishing village where houses lean into each other and the smell of drying fish hangs in the air. Slower here. Older. Easier to breathe.

## The Big Buddha

Up the cable car from Tung Chung, the clouds thinning as you climb. The Tian Tan Buddha sits at the top, thirty-four meters of bronze, looking out over nothing and everything.
